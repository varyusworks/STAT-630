About Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

What is CKD?

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) refers to the loss of kidney function which occurs gradually over time. The kidneys' primary function is to remove excess fluids and wastes from your blood to be excreted out in urine. Kidneys also produce hormones that help regulate blood pressure, red blood cell production, and bone health. As CKD progresses, dangerous amounts of fluid, electrolytes, and wastes can be accrued. CKD can lead to other health issues, including kidney failure, which can be fatal. You may have few signs or symptoms in the early stages of CKD. It is possible that you would not notice until your kidney function has deteriorated dramatically. CKD treatment focuses on slowing the progression of kidney damage, which is normally accomplished by addressing the underlying cause. The sooner you know that you have CKD, the sooner you can start taking precautions to protect your kidneys.

What are additional major risk factors and/or causes of CKD?

  • Diabetes
  • High blood pressure
  • Heart disease
  • Family history of kidney failure
  • Heart and blood vessel (cardiovascular) disease
  • Smoking
  • Obesity
  • Family history of kidney disease
  • Abnormal kidney structure
  • Older age

According to research, CKD is more common in African Americans, Hispanics, and American Indians due to higher rates of diabetes and high blood pressure.

What are the symptoms of CKD?

Symptoms of early CKD may be absent. Kidney damage generally develops slowly, accumulating over time. For many, blood and urine tests are the only way to determine whether they have CKD. The following are some of the signs and symptoms as the disease advances:

  • chest pain
  • dry skin
  • itching or numbness
  • fatigue
  • headaches
  • increased or decreased urination
  • loss of appetite
  • muscle cramps
  • nausea
  • shortness of breath
  • sleep problems
  • trouble concentrating
  • vomiting
  • high blood pressure (hypertension)

How can you lower your risk?

  • Make healthy food decisions and consider consulting a dietitian
  • Include physical exercise in your daily routine
  • Aim for and maintain a healthy weight
  • Get enough sleep
  • Quit smoking
  • Look into stress-relieving practices
  • Manage diabetes, high blood pressure, and heart disease
  • Talk to your health-care provider

You may want to inquire about your kidney health at your next doctor's appointment. Your health care professional will assist you in determining how often you should be screened
